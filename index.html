2025–26 roster context

Of 861 players from 2024-25, 516 returned — a 60% retention rate, an improvement over the prior year’s 57%. The net roster decline of 67 players reflects a gap in new registrations: 278 new players joined in 2025-26 versus 384 the prior year. Of the 345 total departures, 79 were members of the U19 cohort completing their final year of eligibility — a natural and expected outcome.

A structural shift in age composition

The first two seasons carried an exceptionally large contingent of high school-age players — 506 players were U14 or older in 2021-22, representing 58% of the roster. That included 111 U18 players and large cohorts of 2005 and 2006 birth year players who have since graduated through the program.

As those cohorts completed their eligibility, the program has shifted younger. Players in the U9–U13 age groups have grown from 360 to 396 over five seasons — a net gain of 36 players. The foundation of the program is broader today than it was at founding.

△

Five-season program indicators

Retention rate has remained stable across five seasons and improved in 2025-26. The younger age groups that represent long-term program health are growing. 65% of the current roster has been with Valor for two or more consecutive seasons. The primary area of focus heading into 2026-27 is new player acquisition, particularly at the U9 and U10 level where families typically enter competitive youth soccer.

Not all new players are the same

When we say 278 players are new this season, that covers two very different groups.
—
First-Time Travel Players
Kids playing travel soccer for the very first time anywhere — Valor's U9 entry class. These players were not old enough to be on any roster last year, so they are genuinely brand new to the sport at this level.
—
Transfers from Other Clubs
Experienced travel players who chose to leave another club and join Valor. Each one is a competitive win — a family that evaluated their options and chose us.
These two groups are often lumped together as simply "new players" — but they represent very different things. First-time travel players are a pipeline investment. Transfers are a signal of the club's competitive reputation in the region.

60% returning year-over-year is normal and healthy for competitive travel soccer

Youth travel clubs see natural turnover every year — players age out, families relocate, kids try new sports. A club retaining 55–70% of its players annually is in a strong position. Valor has been in that range for five consecutive seasons.

Every season, a new wave of families joins as the youngest age group enters travel soccer for the first time. That natural cycle is what keeps any youth club healthy and growing.

Girls & Boys Programs

The girls and boys programs have performed differently over five seasons — both are strong, with some areas of opportunity.

👧

Girls have returned at a higher rate every single season

The girls program retains 67–68% of players in most seasons — a mark of strong team culture and program quality. Girls enrollment held steady at 364 players for four straight seasons, one of the most consistent numbers in the data.

👦

The boys program is an opportunity area worth exploring

Boys retention has held around 54–62% across five seasons — healthy, though consistently lower than girls. This season both programs saw slight enrollment decreases. Understanding what drives the girls program's stronger loyalty could inform how we support the boys program.

By Age Group

How each age group has performed over time — and what the data tells us about the club's long-term health.

A quick note on how age groups work: In youth travel soccer, players are organized by birth year. "Born in 2015" means those players are currently around age 10 — their age group is called U11 (under 11). Younger birth years = currently older players. Each birth year represents one age group. Players enter travel soccer at U9 (their first season ever) and typically play through U18 or U19.
🌱

The youngest age groups are Valor's most loyal — a great sign for the future

Players born in 2013, 2014, and 2015 — currently U12 through U14 — return at 70–72% on average every season. These are the families who have committed to the club for the long haul. They will be with us for 4–6 more years.

🎓

Older players aging out is expected and healthy

The oldest age group in our data (born 2007, currently U19) only returned at 18% this season — because most of them finished their youth soccer careers. This is normal. Every sport has this natural graduation cycle. It is not attrition; it is success.

Team Levels & Player Movement

How Valor's competitive structure works, and how players move between levels each season.

How Valor's team levels work: Within each age group, Valor fields multiple teams at different competitive levels. The most competitive is ECNL (a top national league). Below that is Pre-ECNL, then Gold, White, Black, and Silver. Players are placed based on tryout performance each spring. This structure means a player can move up, stay at the same level, or occasionally be placed on a lower team based on their development relative to their peers.
📈

More than a third of returning players earned a promotion this season

Of the 516 players who came back in 2025-26, 187 (36%) were placed on a higher-level team than the year before. That is development working exactly as it should.

✅

Players moving down a level is rare, normal, and consistent

About 1 in 12 returning players moves to a lower team in any given season (8% this year). This has been consistent at 8–13% for five seasons. It reflects honest, fair placement based on tryouts — not a concern.
